# 07 - Deklination (Article & Adjective Declension)

## üìö Basic Concept

**Deklination** refers to how articles, adjectives, and pronouns change their endings based on:
1. **Gender** (masculine, feminine, neuter)
2. **Number** (singular, plural)
3. **Case** (Nominativ, Akkusativ, Dativ, Genitiv)

In German, adjectives that come **before a noun** must take specific endings that match the gender, number, and case of the noun.

**Examples:**
- **Der** gut**e** Mann (Nominativ masculine)
- **Den** gut**en** Mann (Akkusativ masculine)
- **Ein** gut**er** Mann (Nominativ masculine with indefinite article)

---

## üéØ Why Is Deklination Important?

Deklination is essential because:
- It makes your German **grammatically correct**
- It helps listeners/readers understand **which noun** you're describing
- It's **heavily tested** on the B1 TELC exam
- It's a marker of **intermediate/advanced German**

**Wrong adjective ending = grammatical mistake!**

---

## üìç Three Types of Adjective Declension

### Type 1: **Strong Declension** (No Article)
When there is **NO article** before the adjective

### Type 2: **Weak Declension** (Definite Article)
When there is a **definite article** (der, die, das, etc.)

### Type 3: **Mixed Declension** (Indefinite Article)
When there is an **indefinite article** (ein, eine, kein, mein, etc.)

---

## üîß Type 1: Strong Declension (No Article)

**When to use:** When there is **no article** before the adjective.

The adjective takes "strong" endings because it must show the gender and case (since there's no article to do it).

### Strong Declension Table

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Nom** | gut**er** Wein | gut**e** Milch | gut**es** Bier | gut**e** √Ñpfel |
| **Akk** | gut**en** Wein | gut**e** Milch | gut**es** Bier | gut**e** √Ñpfel |
| **Dat** | gut**em** Wein | gut**er** Milch | gut**em** Bier | gut**en** √Ñpfeln |
| **Gen** | gut**en** Weins | gut**er** Milch | gut**en** Biers | gut**er** √Ñpfel |

### Examples:

1. **Guter** Wein schmeckt mir. (Nom masc)
   *Good wine tastes good to me.*

2. Ich trinke **kalte** Milch. (Akk fem)
   *I drink cold milk.*

3. Das Haus ist aus **rotem** Backstein. (Dat masc)
   *The house is made of red brick.*

**Note:** Strong declension is less common at B1 level, but important for food/drink descriptions!

## üîß Type 2: Weak Declension (Definite Article: der, die, das)

**When to use:** After **definite articles** (der, die, das, die) and words that decline like them (dieser, jener, welcher, jeder, alle).

The article already shows gender and case, so the adjective takes "weak" (simpler) endings.

### Weak Declension Table

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Nom** | d**er** gut**e** Mann | d**ie** gut**e** Frau | d**as** gut**e** Kind | d**ie** gut**en** Kinder |
| **Akk** | d**en** gut**en** Mann | d**ie** gut**e** Frau | d**as** gut**e** Kind | d**ie** gut**en** Kinder |
| **Dat** | d**em** gut**en** Mann | d**er** gut**en** Frau | d**em** gut**en** Kind | d**en** gut**en** Kindern |
| **Gen** | d**es** gut**en** Mannes | d**er** gut**en** Frau | d**es** gut**en** Kindes | d**er** gut**en** Kinder |

### Pattern Recognition:

**Simple rule:**
- **Nominative singular:** -**e** (all genders)
- **ALL other cases:** -**en**

### Examples:

1. **Der** nett**e** Mann wohnt hier. (Nom masc)
   *The nice man lives here.*

2. Ich sehe **den** nett**en** Mann. (Akk masc)
   *I see the nice man.*

3. Ich helfe **der** nett**en** Frau. (Dat fem)
   *I help the nice woman.*

4. **Das** sch√∂n**e** Haus ist teuer. (Nom neut)
   *The beautiful house is expensive.*

5. Wir besuchen **die** alt**en** Freunde. (Akk plural)
   *We visit the old friends.*

## üîß Type 3: Mixed Declension (Indefinite Article: ein, eine, kein, mein)

**When to use:** After **indefinite articles** (ein, eine) and **possessive pronouns** (mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser, euer, ihr) and **kein**.

Called "mixed" because it combines features of both strong and weak declension.

### Mixed Declension Table

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Nom** | ein gut**er** Mann | ein**e** gut**e** Frau | ein gut**es** Kind | - gut**e** Kinder |
| **Akk** | ein**en** gut**en** Mann | ein**e** gut**e** Frau | ein gut**es** Kind | - gut**e** Kinder |
| **Dat** | ein**em** gut**en** Mann | ein**er** gut**en** Frau | ein**em** gut**en** Kind | - gut**en** Kindern |
| **Gen** | ein**es** gut**en** Mannes | ein**er** gut**en** Frau | ein**es** gut**en** Kindes | - gut**er** Kinder |

**Note:** There is no plural form of "ein" - use strong declension for plural with no article!

### Pattern Recognition:

**Key insight:** When the article ending is "weak" (e.g., ein, ein, eine), the adjective must be "strong" (-er, -es, -e).

### Examples with "ein":

1. **Ein** nett**er** Mann wohnt hier. (Nom masc)
   *A nice man lives here.*

2. Ich sehe **einen** nett**en** Mann. (Akk masc)
   *I see a nice man.*

3. Ich helfe **einer** nett**en** Frau. (Dat fem)
   *I help a nice woman.*

4. **Ein** sch√∂n**es** Haus ist teuer. (Nom neut)
   *A beautiful house is expensive.*

### Examples with Possessive Pronouns (mein, dein, sein, etc.):

1. **Mein** alt**er** Freund kommt. (Nom masc)
   *My old friend is coming.*

2. Ich besuche **meinen** alt**en** Freund. (Akk masc)
   *I visit my old friend.*

3. Das ist **deine** neu**e** Tasche. (Nom fem)
   *That is your new bag.*

4. Er gibt **seinem** klein**en** Bruder das Buch. (Dat masc)
   *He gives the book to his little brother.*

### Examples with "kein":

1. Ich habe **kein** frisch**es** Brot. (Akk neut)
   *I have no fresh bread.*

2. Ich sehe **keine** interessant**en** Filme. (Akk plural)
   *I don't see any interesting films.*

## üéì Special Cases & Tips

### 1. Multiple Adjectives

When you have **multiple adjectives** before a noun, they all take the **same ending**!

**Examples:**

1. Der **gro√üe**, **starke** Mann arbeitet hier.
   *The big, strong man works here.*

2. Ich sehe einen **jungen**, **netten** Mann.
   *I see a young, nice man.*

3. Sie hat eine **kleine**, **s√º√üe** Katze.
   *She has a small, sweet cat.*

### 2. Adjectives After the Noun (Predicative)

When the adjective comes **after the verb sein/werden**, it does **NOT** change!

**Examples:**

1. Der Mann ist **nett**. (no ending!)
   *The man is nice.*

2. Das Haus ist **sch√∂n**. (no ending!)
   *The house is beautiful.*

3. Die Kinder sind **gl√ºcklich**. (no ending!)
   *The children are happy.*

**Only adjectives BEFORE the noun need declension endings!**

### 3. Adjectives Ending in -el and -er

When adjectives end in **-el** or **-er**, they drop the **-e-** when adding an ending:

| Adjective | Before Ending | With Ending |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| dunkel (dark) | dunk**e**l | ein dunk**l**es Zimmer |
| teuer (expensive) | teu**e**r | ein teu**r**es Auto |
| sauer (sour) | sau**e**r | sau**r**e Milch |

### 4. Adjectives from City Names

Adjectives made from city names add **-er** and **never change**:

**Examples:**

1. Berliner Luft (Berlin air)
2. M√ºnchner Bier (Munich beer)
3. Wiener Schnitzel (Viennese schnitzel)
4. Frankfurter W√ºrstchen (Frankfurt sausages)

**The -er ending stays the same in all cases!**

### 5. Adjective Nouns

Sometimes adjectives are used as nouns. They still follow declension rules!

**Examples:**

1. **Der** Deutsch**e** (the German man)
2. **Die** Deutsch**e** (the German woman)
3. **Ein** Deutsch**er** (a German man)
4. **Eine** Deutsch**e** (a German woman)
5. **Der** Kranke (the sick person)
6. **Der** Alte (the old man)
