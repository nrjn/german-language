# 02 - Relativsatz (Relative Clauses)

## üìö Basic Concept

A **Relativsatz** (relative clause) is a **subordinate clause** that gives additional information about a noun in the main sentence. It answers questions like "Which one?" or "What kind?" and makes your German sound more natural and fluent.

**Key features:**
- Always introduced by a **relative pronoun** (der, die, das, etc.)
- Always a **Nebensatz** (subordinate clause) ‚Üí **verb goes to the end!**
- Always separated by **commas**
- Gives extra, non-essential information (optional but helpful)

---

## üéØ Why Are Relative Clauses Used?

Relative clauses allow you to:
- **Combine two simple sentences** into one complex sentence
- **Add descriptive details** without starting a new sentence
- **Sound more sophisticated** and achieve B1-level fluency
- **Avoid repetition** of nouns

**Example:**
- Without Relativsatz: *Der Mann wohnt in Berlin. Der Mann besucht mich.*
- With Relativsatz: *Der Mann, der in Berlin wohnt, besucht mich.*
  *(The man who lives in Berlin is visiting me.)*

---

## üìç Where Are Relative Clauses Used?

Relative clauses appear:
- In **written German** (emails, letters, reports)
- In **spoken German** (to sound educated and fluent)
- In **B1 exam tasks** (especially Schreiben and Sprechen sections)
- After any **noun** you want to describe further

---

## üîß How to Use Relative Clauses

### Structure

**Main Clause + , + Relative Pronoun + ... + Verb + .**

**Example:**
Das ist der Mann, **der** in Berlin wohnt**.**
*(That is the man who lives in Berlin.)*

### Step-by-Step Formation

**Step 1:** Identify the noun you want to describe (the reference word)
**Step 2:** Choose the correct relative pronoun (matching gender and number)
**Step 3:** Determine the case (Nominativ, Akkusativ, Dativ, Genitiv)
**Step 4:** Build the relative clause with the verb at the end

---

## üìä Relative Pronouns Table

Relative pronouns look almost identical to definite articles (der, die, das), with a few exceptions!

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|---------|
| **Nominativ** | der | die | das | die |
| **Akkusativ** | den | die | das | die |
| **Dativ** | dem | der | dem | denen |
| **Genitiv** | dessen | deren | dessen | deren |

**Key differences from articles:**
- Dativ Plural: **denen** (not den!)
- Genitiv: **dessen** (masculine/neuter), **deren** (feminine/plural)

## üéì Special Rules & Tips

### 1. Relative Clauses with Prepositions

When the relative clause contains a **preposition**, the preposition comes BEFORE the relative pronoun!

**Structure:** Preposition + Relative Pronoun + ... + Verb

**Examples:**

1. Das ist die Stadt, **in der** ich wohne.
   *That is the city in which I live.*

2. Der Mann, **mit dem** ich gesprochen habe, ist nett.
   *The man with whom I spoke is nice.*

3. Die Firma, **f√ºr die** sie arbeitet, ist sehr gro√ü.
   *The company for which she works is very large.*

4. Das Projekt, **an dem** wir arbeiten, ist wichtig.
   *The project on which we are working is important.*

**Important:** The case of the relative pronoun depends on the preposition!
- **mit** ‚Üí Dativ ‚Üí mit dem, mit der, mit denen
- **f√ºr** ‚Üí Akkusativ ‚Üí f√ºr den, f√ºr die
- **in** ‚Üí depends on context (usually Dativ for location)

### 2. Relative Clauses with "WO"

For **places/locations**, you can use **wo** instead of "in dem/in der":

**Examples:**

1. Das ist die Stadt, **wo** ich geboren bin.
   = Das ist die Stadt, **in der** ich geboren bin.
   *That is the city where I was born.*

2. Das Restaurant, **wo** wir gegessen haben, war teuer.
   = Das Restaurant, **in dem** wir gegessen haben, war teuer.
   *The restaurant where we ate was expensive.*

**Note:** Using "wo" is more casual; "in dem/in der" is more formal.

### 3. Relative Clauses with "WAS"

Use **was** as the relative pronoun when referring to:
- **Indefinite pronouns:** etwas, nichts, alles, vieles
- **Whole sentences/ideas**
- **Superlatives used as nouns:** das Beste, das Sch√∂nste

**Examples:**

1. Alles, **was** er sagt, ist falsch.
   *Everything that he says is wrong.*

2. Ich habe etwas gekauft, **was** sehr teuer war.
   *I bought something that was very expensive.*

3. Das ist das Beste, **was** mir passiert ist.
   *That is the best thing that happened to me.*

4. Sie hat die Pr√ºfung bestanden, **was** mich sehr freut.
   *She passed the exam, which makes me very happy.*

### 4. Verb Position in Relative Clauses

**CRITICAL RULE:** The conjugated verb ALWAYS goes to the END of the relative clause!

**Examples:**

‚úÖ Der Mann, der in Berlin **wohnt**, ist nett.
‚ùå Der Mann, der wohnt in Berlin, ist nett.

‚úÖ Das Buch, das ich gestern **gekauft habe**, ist gut.
‚ùå Das Buch, das ich habe gestern gekauft, ist gut.

### 5. Commas with Relative Clauses

Relative clauses are ALWAYS set off by commas:
- Comma BEFORE the relative clause
- Comma AFTER the relative clause (if the sentence continues)

**Example:**
Der Mann**,** der in Berlin wohnt**,** besucht mich morgen.
